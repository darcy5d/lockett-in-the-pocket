## The Foundation Era (1908–1981) {#the-foundation-era}

### NSW — Where It All Began

Rugby league in Australia was born in New South Wales. In August 1907, disaffected rugby union players met in Sydney to break away over unpaid compensation and lost wages. The **New South Wales Rugby Football League (NSWRFL)** was formed, and the first Sydney premiership kicked off on 20 April **1908** with nine foundation clubs.

```
NSWRFL Premiership (1908)
├── First Grade (top-tier club competition)
├── Reserve Grade (1908 onwards — run alongside first grade by same clubs)
└── Third Grade (1908 – ~1980s, eventually discontinued)
```

**Reserve Grade** from its very first season in 1908 was run almost exclusively as reserve squads of the same NSWRFL clubs, playing in the same colours. North Sydney Bears are the only club to have competed in every single season since 1908 — an unbroken 100+ year record.

A **Third Grade** competition also ran from 1908 through to the 1980s before being phased out.

### Mid-Week Cup Competitions

In **1974**, the NSWRFL launched the **Amco Cup** — a popular mid-week knockout competition featuring clubs from the main premiership and eventually interstate sides. It ran for 16 seasons under various naming sponsors before being discontinued after 1989:

```
Mid-Week Cup Lineage:
Amco Cup (1974–1981)
  └── Tooth Cup (1982–1983)
        └── National Panasonic Cup / KB Cup (1984–1987)
              └── Panasonic Cup (1988–1989)
                    └── [discontinued 1989]
```

> **Note:** Often listed as ending with the National Panasonic Cup in 1987 — but the competition ran two further seasons as the Panasonic Cup (1988–1989) before being discontinued. The Winfield Cup name was used for the main first-grade premiership from 1982, not the mid-week cup.

This mid-week format helped grow rugby league beyond Sydney and was the first real exposure for interstate expansion.

### The Winfield Cup Era Begins

In **1982**, the NSWRFL rebranded its main first-grade competition as the **Winfield Cup** (commercial tobacco sponsorship), and made two landmark moves:

- **Canberra Raiders** admitted (ACT)
- **Illawarra Steelers** admitted (southern NSW)

This was the beginning of the end for a purely Sydney-centric competition.

## The Expansion Era (1982–1994) {#the-expansion-era}

### NSW Competition Grows Nationally

Through the 1980s, the competition expanded dramatically beyond its Sydney heartland:

| Year | New Clubs Added |
|------|----------------|
| 1982 | Canberra Raiders, Illawarra Steelers |
| 1988 | Brisbane Broncos, Gold Coast-Tweed Giants, Newcastle Knights |
| 1995 | North Queensland Cowboys, South Queensland Crushers, Western Reds (Perth), Auckland Warriors |

By **1994**, the competition had outgrown its NSWRFL identity. Control of the first-grade competition formally passed to the **Australian Rugby League (ARL)** — a broader national body — for the 1995 season. The NSWRL retained responsibility for state-level administration (NSW Cup, junior reps, State of Origin).

### ARL Premiership (1995–1997)

```
ARL Premiership (1995–1997)
├── First Grade (20 teams by 1995 — national competition)
├── Reserve Grade (run alongside first grade)
└── Under-19 / Youth competitions
```

### Reserve Grade in This Era

Through 1908–1996, **Reserve Grade** remained purely a second-string competition attached to NSWRL/ARL clubs — no standalone clubs, no separate identity. It was simply "the reserves."

## The Super League War (1995–1997) {#the-super-league-war}

### Background

In 1995, News Corporation (Rupert Murdoch) attempted to purchase the pay television rights to Australian rugby league. When rebuffed by the ARL and Kerry Packer's Optus Vision, News Corp went nuclear — signing NRL clubs directly to breakaway contracts and launching a rival competition: **Super League Australia**.

Two years of brutal legal battles followed before both competitions ran simultaneously in **1997**.

### Split Competition Structure — 1997

```
1997 DUAL COMPETITION STRUCTURE
│
├── ARL Competition (13 clubs — traditional ARL aligned)
│   ├── First Grade (ARL Premiership / "Optus Cup")
│   ├── Reserve Grade → renamed "Presidents Cup"
│   └── Youth grades
│
└── Super League Australia (10 clubs — News Corp aligned)
    ├── Senior Grade → "Telstra Cup" (first grade only)
    ├── Reserve Grade → still called "Reserve Grade" (separate from Telstra Cup)
    ├── Under-19s → "Mal Meninga Cup"
    └── Under-17s → "Terry Lamb Cup"
```

**Key detail on NSW Reserve Grade split:**
- The ARL's reserve grade was renamed the **Presidents Cup** for 1997
- The Super League reserve grade was still called **Reserve Grade** (won by Canterbury Bulldogs)
- This created a bizarre situation where two separate reserve/second-grade competitions ran in the same city simultaneously

### Super League's Competition (1997 Only)

The 10 Super League clubs were predominantly Queensland and interstate-based — Brisbane Broncos, Cronulla, Penrith, Canberra, North Queensland, Hunter Mariners (new), Adelaide Rams (new), Perth Reds, Auckland Warriors, and the South Queensland Crushers.

Brisbane Broncos dominated — winning 14 of 18 rounds, defeating Cronulla in the Grand Final in Brisbane (the first Australian top-level grand final held outside Sydney).

### Peace Deal — Late 1997

The financial drain on both sides was catastrophic. On 23 September 1997, the ARL announced plans for a unified competition. By 19 December, at the Sydney Football Stadium, club representatives voted 36–4 to accept News Limited's settlement offer.

The **National Rugby League (NRL)** was formed as a joint venture between the ARL and News Limited.

## NRL Unification (1998–2007) {#nrl-unification}

### The Inaugural NRL — 1998

The 1998 NRL season launched with **20 teams** — 19 surviving ARL/Super League clubs plus the newly created Melbourne Storm (a News Limited expansion franchise).

Several clubs were immediately closed due to financial ruin from the war:
- Hunter Mariners (Super League — folded)
- Perth Reds (Super League — $10M in debt, folded)
- South Queensland Crushers (ARL — folded)

By end of 1998, two more clubs were closed despite finishing the season:
- Adelaide Rams (Super League)
- Gold Coast Chargers (ARL — ironically one of the few clubs that turned a profit during the war)

```
NRL 1998 — Competition Structure
├── NRL First Grade (20 teams → reducing over time)
├── Reserve Grade / "Presidents Cup" (NSW clubs, NSWRL administered)
└── Under-19 / Youth competitions
```

### Club Mergers (1999–2002)

The post-war rationalisation saw several clubs fold or merge under NRL direction to reduce the oversaturation:

| Clubs Merged | New Entity | Year |
|---|---|---|
| St George Dragons + Illawarra Steelers | St George Illawarra Dragons | 2000 season |
| Balmain Tigers + Western Suburbs Magpies | Wests Tigers | 2000 season |
| North Sydney Bears + Manly Warringah | Northern Eagles | 2000 season (demerged 2002) |
| South Sydney Rabbitohs | Expelled from NRL 1999 | Returned 2002 via court victory |

The Northern Eagles experiment lasted only three seasons (2000–2002) before Manly returned as a standalone club. North Sydney Bears dropped to the NSW Cup level, where they remain today.

### NSW Reserve Grade Restructure

With the mergers creating orphaned clubs and the competition landscape changing, the NSWRL restructured the competition below NRL level:

```
NSW Below-NRL Competition Lineage:
Reserve Grade (1908–1996)
  └── Presidents Cup (1997 — ARL side during split)
        └── NSWRL First Division (1998–2001)
              └── NSWRL Premier League (2002–2007)
                    └── NSW Cup (2008–2018)
                          └── Canterbury Cup NSW / Intrust Super Premiership NSW (2019–2020)
                                └── Knock-On Effect NSW Cup (2021–present)
```

**Key change in 2002–2007 (Premier League era):** The competition began allowing standalone clubs — not just NRL reserve squads. Merged NRL clubs' former identities (e.g. North Sydney Bears, Newtown Jets, Western Suburbs, Balmain) re-emerged as standalone Premier League/NSW Cup teams. This preserved heritage clubs while the NRL thinned its top-tier roster.

**2008 — Rebranded as NSW Cup:** The competition was formalised under this name, with clear NRL feeder affiliations. From this point, some NRL clubs outsourced their reserve operations to separate affiliated clubs (e.g. Newtown Jets affiliated with the Roosters, Penrith outsourced to Windsor Wolves, Cronulla to Cobras).

**Auckland entry (2007):** The Auckland Lions — later the **Auckland Vulcans** — entered the NSW Cup, providing the Warriors with a reserve grade team in the competition. The Warriors took full control in 2014.

## The Modern Era (2008–Present) {#the-modern-era}

### NSW Cup / Knock-On Effect NSW Cup

By 2008, the NSW Cup had evolved from a pure reserve-grade competition into a genuine second-tier competition featuring a mix of NRL feeder clubs and heritage standalone clubs.

**Current structure (2024–25):**
- 13 teams
- NRL reserve squads (most NSW-based NRL clubs)
- Standalone heritage clubs: North Sydney Bears, Newtown Jets, Western Suburbs
- Interstate: Auckland (Warriors reserve side), Canberra (Raiders affiliate)

**Naming rights history (sponsorship):**

| Period | Competition Name |
|---|---|
| 1908–1996 | Reserve Grade |
| 1997 | Presidents Cup (ARL) / Reserve Grade (Super League — separate) |
| 1998–2001 | NSWRL First Division |
| 2002–2007 | NSWRL Premier League |
| 2008–2017 | NSW Cup / VB NSW Cup |
| 2018 | Intrust Super Premiership NSW |
| 2019–2020 | Canterbury Cup NSW |
| 2021–present | Knock-On Effect NSW Cup |

### Jersey Flegg Cup (Under-21s / Under-20s / Under-19s)

Named after NSWRFL president Jersey Flegg, this under-age competition began in **1961** (not 1965) as an under-19 competition. Its history is not continuous — it was **suspended from 2008 to 2017** when the NRL launched the national under-20s competition (NYC), then revived by the NSWRL in 2018.

```
Jersey Flegg Cup Timeline:
1961–1997  Under-19 (seasonal/short format, part of NSWRL junior rep season)
1998–2007  Under-20 (full season, running alongside NRL)
2008–2017  SUSPENDED — replaced by NRL National Youth Competition (NYC)
2018–2020  Revived as Under-20 by NSWRL
2021–pres  Under-21 format

NYC (National Youth Competition) Timeline:
2008–2012  Toyota Cup (all 16 NRL clubs, national under-20s)
2013–2017  Holden Cup (rebranded, same format)
2018       Discontinued — state-based competitions revived
2026       NRL investigating revival of national under-20/21 competition
```

### NSW Pathway — Full Picture (Modern)

```
NSW Pathway (2008–2017 — NYC era):
NRL First Grade
  └── Knock-On Effect NSW Cup (2nd tier — reserve grade)
        └── National Youth Competition / Toyota Cup / Holden Cup (national U20 — NRL administered)
              └── SG Ball Cup (Under-18s, since 1965)
                    └── Harold Matthews Cup (Under-16s, since 1970)

NSW Pathway (2018–present — post-NYC):
NRL First Grade
  └── Knock-On Effect NSW Cup (2nd tier — reserve grade)
        └── Jersey Flegg Cup (Under-21s, revived 2018)
              └── SG Ball Cup (Under-18s, since 1965)
                    └── Harold Matthews Cup (Under-16s, since 1970)
```

### NRL State Championship (2014–Present)

With both the NSW Cup and QLD Cup (Hostplus Cup) operating as parallel second-tier competitions, the NRL introduced an annual **State Championship** in 2014 — a grand final playoff between the NSW Cup and QLD Cup premiers to determine the best second-tier club in the country.

### NRLW — Women's NRL (2018–Present)

A major missing piece from the original document: the **NRL Women's competition (NRLW)** launched in **2018** with four teams, has since grown to eight teams, and is now a significant part of the national competition structure. The women's pathway mirrors the men's:

```
Women's Pathway (Modern):
NRLW (National — 8 teams, 2024)
  └── Harvey Norman NSW Women's Premiership / Tarsha Gale Cup (NSW)
  └── Harvey Norman QLD Women's Premiership (QLD)
        └── State-based under-19 women's pathways
```

Key women's competition names:
- **NRLW** (2018–present): National competition
- **Harvey Norman NSW Women's Premiership**: State second-tier (NSW)
- **Tarsha Gale Cup**: NSW Under-18 women's
- **Mal Meninga Cup (Women's)**: QLD development

## Queensland — A Parallel Universe (1908–1998) {#queensland-parallel}

### QRL Foundation (1908)

Queensland founded its own rugby league body — the **Queensland Rugby Football League (QRL)** — on 28 February 1908. Like NSW, Queensland had a thriving club competition centred on Brisbane. **RLP data**: QRL competition available from 1909 (`qrl` slug); continues through 1920s (some years use variant structures e.g. "QRL First Round", "QRL Second Round").

### Brisbane Rugby League (BRL) — 1922–1997

In 1922, disgruntled clubs broke away from the QRL to form the **Brisbane Rugby Football League (BRFL)** — later known simply as the BRL. The BRL became Queensland's premier club competition and ran parallel to the NSWRFL competition in the same way:

```
Queensland Competition Structure (1922–1987)
│
├── Brisbane Rugby League (BRL) — premier club competition
│   ├── First Grade
│   ├── Reserve Grade
│   └── Various junior grades
│
└── QRL Statewide competition (from 1982 — Winfield State League)
```

The BRL featured clubs like Broncos (before they joined the NSWRL), Eastern Suburbs, Norths, Wynnum Manly, Redcliffe Dolphins, Ipswich Jets, and many others. It was a thriving standalone competition with loyal support bases. **RLP data**: BRL (`brl` slug) verified from 1930s through 1997.

### The Winfield State League (1982–1995)

In 1982, mirroring the NSWRL's mid-week Amco Cup concept, the QRL launched the **Winfield State League** — a statewide format competition running alongside the BRL, involving clubs from across Queensland (Townsville, Cairns, Mackay, regional centres) rather than just Brisbane.

```
Winfield State League (1982–1995)
  → Statewide Queensland competition
  → Ran alongside the BRL club competition
  → Featured regional Queensland teams not in the BRL
```

This was abolished in 1995–1996 when federal tobacco advertising bans (Tobacco Advertising Prohibition Act 1992) prohibited Winfield from sponsoring sporting events.

### Broncos Enter the NSWRL (1988) — BRL Becomes Second Tier

The pivotal moment for Queensland came in **1988** when the **Brisbane Broncos** and **Gold Coast-Tweed Giants** joined the NSWRL. Almost all of Queensland's elite players — Wally Lewis, Gene Miles, Colin Scott, Greg Dowling, Bryan Niebling and others — defected immediately to the new NRL-level clubs.

The BRL officially remained a "first-tier" competition on paper, but in reality became second-tier from 1988 onward.

```
Queensland Competition Hierarchy Post-1988:
├── NSWRL/ARL/NRL (Brisbane Broncos, Gold Coast, Cowboys from 1995)  ← TOP TIER
└── Brisbane Rugby League (BRL)  ← Effectively second tier from 1988
      └── Winfield State League (statewide, alongside BRL until 1995)
```

### Queensland Cup Launches (1996)

With the Winfield State League gone and the BRL in structural decline, the QRL launched the **Queensland Cup** in **1996** as a replacement — a proper statewide second-tier competition designed to integrate NRL club affiliations and develop players for the national competition.

**Inaugural 1996 Queensland Cup had 16 teams:**
Bundaberg Grizzlies, Cairns Cyclones, Central Capras, Eastern Suburbs Tigers, Ipswich Jets, Logan City Scorpions, Mackay Sea-Eagles, Norths Devils, Pine Rivers Brothers, Port Moresby Vipers, Redcliffe Dolphins, Souths Magpies, Sunshine Coast Falcons, Toowoomba Clydesdales, Wests Panthers, Wynnum Seagulls.

The QLD Cup ran alongside the BRL for 1996 and 1997.

### BRL Folds (End of 1997)

The Brisbane Rugby League competition concluded after the **1997 season**, making the Queensland Cup the uncontested premier club competition in Queensland from **1998** onwards. From this point, the BRL was revived only in 2015 as a reserve-grade competition below the Queensland Cup.

```
Queensland Competition Lineage:
BRL (1922–1997) ─────────────────────────────────────────────────── [ENDED 1997]
Winfield State League (1982–1995) ───────────────────────────────── [ENDED 1995]
Queensland Cup (1996–present)
  ├── Originally alongside BRL (1996–1997)
  └── Premier QLD competition from 1998
```

### Queensland Cup — Naming Rights History

| Period | Competition Name |
|---|---|
| 1996–1999 | Queensland Cup |
| 2000–2009 | Bundaberg Rum Cup / Bundy Gold Cup (Bundaberg Rum naming rights throughout) |
| 2010–2021 | Intrust Super Cup |
| 2022–present | Hostplus Cup |

> **Note:** "Channel 9 Cup" is sometimes cited for certain years in this period but appears to reflect broadcast rights terminology rather than official competition naming. Bundaberg Rum held the naming rights continuously from 2000 through the end of 2009.

### QLD Under-20s Development Pathway

The QLD equivalent of the Jersey Flegg Cup has had an interrupted history mirroring NSW:

- From 2008–2017, QLD NRL clubs participated in the national **NYC (Toyota/Holden Cup)** rather than a state-based under-20s competition.
- When the NYC was abolished after 2017, the QRL introduced the **Hastings Deering Colts** (Under-20s) in **2018** as a replacement.
- As of 2025, the Hastings Deering Colts has been **scrapped**, leaving a gap in QLD's under-21 pathways that the NRL is attempting to fill with a planned revival of a national under-20/21 competition from 2026.

```
QLD Pathway (2018–2024):
NRL First Grade (Broncos, Cowboys, Titans, Dolphins)
  └── Hostplus Cup / Queensland Cup (2nd tier)
        └── Hastings Deering Colts (Under-20s, 2018–2024, now discontinued)
              └── BRL A-Grade / FOGS Cup (Brisbane reserve grade)
                    └── Junior representative competitions

QLD Pathway (2025):
NRL First Grade
  └── Hostplus Cup (2nd tier)
        └── [Under-20s gap — NRL national comp revival planned for 2026]
              └── BRL / FOGS Cup / regional comps
```

  - **Folded (players dispersed)**: Hunter Mariners, Perth Reds, Adelaide Rams, South Queensland Crushers, Gold Coast Chargers
  - **New**: Melbourne Storm (1998 expansion)
- Expect variance at cutover years; model settles over the medium term.

### Parse-Then-Era Approach

1. **Scrape**: Parse all discoverable competitions from RLP calendar per year; store raw data with slug, year, and structure.
2. **Accept gaps**: No requirement for complete coverage before processing (e.g. some years may lack round data or use variant structures).
3. **Post-process**: Work resultant data into eras (scoring eras, competition lineage, tier mapping).
